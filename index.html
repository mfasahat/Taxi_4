Where to Taxi?

Narrative

Chicago, Illinois is the third largest city in America, boasting a resident population totaling nearly three million people spread over 234 square miles. The city consistently ranks among the top 10 most visited cities in the country and has two international airports serving the city, allowing around 55 million tourists to visit annually. Such a large influx of people into the city generates a high demand for travel and requires an efficient system to get people where they need to go. Arguably, the city of Chicago’s most iconic means of transportation is, and remains, the taxi.

For a taxi company where efficiency is a necessity in maintaining profitability, knowing how to maximize their fleet’s cost of time is essential for successful business operations. By analyzing taxi trip data, made publicly available by the City of Chicago, we venture to analyze the time spent “off the clock” between pick up and drop offs. By making inferences as to the activities taxi drivers partake of during these times can better help the industry in quantifying data such as time asleep, meal times, off days, and busiest working days/hours.

Our approach to analyze the complexity of this condition was to first identify the time that was spent in between pickup and drop off’s, where the taxi was not collecting revenue, this we consider “idle” time. Time either spent eating a meal, driving to another location, days taken off, etc. After breaking our data set down to reflect these idle times, we analysed the data looking for outliers, these peaks will form the main basis for our following assumptions. These assumptions are meant to infer on how the taxi driver spends their time, because time in the taxi industry of Chicago, means money.

Findings + Conclusion

After having analyzed our data set visually, we can draw relative correlations and probable next steps moving forward. The first correlation between our results is that the more popular areas for taxi use are centered around the two international airports serving Chicago and the immediate downtown known as “the Loop.” These areas likely receive more taxi trips due to constant yearly tourism and business. This knowledge in relation to an earlier question we’ve proposed, we can infer that should taxis serve only these popular locations, fleet efficiency would be maximized to cover them, while seeing an increase in revenue as taxis would have probable cause to assume a trip when in close proximity.

The second correlation to the data can be made by analyzing the time of day in relation to each pick up or drop off location. The times where locations are busiest, remain constant from the beginning to end of the work day. This supports the idea that if taxis serve only these certain locations each day, that profit would remain constant, having no lull period during the day due to a lesser demand in taxi services, inferring both that the demand for taxis to serve these areas are high, while other parts of the city receive less popularity and therefore less revenue.

While our data set provides us with the necessary information to observe these correlations, our next steps will be to develop an agent based simulation or model. This would allow us to examine specific variables that would benefit or challenge our assumptions as a result of our research. An example of such a simulation could be utilizing the distance between each popular destination, in relation to taxi fare and taxi demand. Allowing us to look at the distance traveled between these destinations, find the optimal efficiency of taxi distribution throughout the day at any given specific location and profitability.
